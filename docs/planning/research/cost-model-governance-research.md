## Synthesis — the levers, who ships them, and where they break

Cost/model governance for agent fleets has converged on **four stacked levers**, each mapping to a vault candidate, each with its own adoption curve and its own documented failure class:

| Mechanism | What it does | Who ships it (representative) | Adoption signal | Where it breaks |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Model right-sizing ladder** (`pattern-model-right-sizing-ladder`) | Assign tiered models to tiered work: strong model plans/orchestrates, cheaper model executes/runs subtasks | Anthropic multi-agent research system (Opus lead + Sonnet subagents); Claude Code Opus-plan/Sonnet-execute; OpenAI tier ladder (o-series/GPT-5 reasoning vs mini/nano) | First-party in the two largest coding agents; Anthropic reports the ladder beat single-agent Opus by **90.2%** on internal research eval | Multi-agent ladders burn **~15× the tokens** of chat; cheap-execution tier triggers retry loops ("false economy") that erase the saving |
| **Routing / cascades** (`concept-routing-planning-to-stronger-models`) | Per-request classifier or cascade picks cheap-vs-strong model; planning/hard → strong, routine → cheap | RouteLLM (OSS), FrugalGPT (cascade), Martian, OpenRouter Auto Router (NotDiamond), Unify, GPT-5 internal router | RouteLLM: **>85% cost cut on MT-Bench at 95% of GPT-4 quality**; FrugalGPT: **up to 98% cost cut** matching GPT-4; OpenRouter routes across **300+ models**; Martian nearing **$1.3B** valuation | **Misroute = silent quality regression**: hard task sent to weak model; routers shown to *degenerately converge* to one model; benchmark cost-savings overstate real-world savings |
| **Prompt / context caching** (`concept-prompt-caching`) | Charge a discounted rate for repeated prompt prefixes (system prompt, tool defs, long context) | Anthropic (explicit `cache_control`), OpenAI (automatic), Google Gemini (explicit + implicit) | Anthropic: **0.10× read = 90% off**; OpenAI: **automatic, 50% off cached input**; Gemini: **0.25× = 75% off**; field reports of **59–90% bill cuts** | Cache **misses** from non-deterministic prefixes (timestamps), invalidation cascades (change tools → whole cache dies), 5-min TTL expiry, exact-match requirement |
| **Token-spend budgeting** (`concept-token-spend-has-four-quadrants`) | Deliberately allocate spend: reasoning-effort / thinking-budget dials, per-task token caps, spend guardrails, cost-aware loops | OpenAI `reasoning_effort` (minimal→xhigh); Anthropic extended-thinking budget; OpenRouter reasoning tokens; FinOps-for-AI budget guardrails | `reasoning_effort` shipped across GPT-5 family; FinOps Foundation stood up a **"FinOps for AI"** working group | **Reasoning-effort miscalibration** (over/under-thinking); **budget caps truncate mid-task**; per-call tagging is hard because "an LLM call is a transaction, not an asset" |

Four cross-cutting findings stand out:

**1. The right-sizing ladder is real and shipped — but its savings are conditional, not automatic.** The canonical evidence is Anthropic's own multi-agent research system: **Claude Opus 4 as lead orchestrator + Claude Sonnet 4 subagents outperformed single-agent Opus 4 by 90.2%** on an internal research eval, with token usage alone explaining **~80% of the performance variance** ([anthropic.com/engineering/multi-agent-research-system](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/multi-agent-research-system)). The same writeup is blunt that this architecture **consumes ~15× the tokens of a normal chat**, so it is "only worth it for high-value tasks." The ladder is therefore a *value-routing* decision, not a *cost-reduction* decision — it spends more to get more. The cheap-tier-for-execution half of the ladder has a documented inversion: cheaper per-token models can trigger **5–10 self-correction cycles** to land a task, and "stronger models reduce iteration overhead rather than total cost" — the false economy ([dr-arsanjani.medium.com](https://dr-arsanjani.medium.com/the-economic-fallacy-of-cheap-ai-the-real-costs-of-agentic-systems-936618305706)).

**2. Routing's headline savings come from benchmarks; the failure mode is a silent quality regression.** RouteLLM (arXiv **2406.18665**) reports **>85% cost reduction on MT-Bench, ~45% on MMLU, ~35% on GSM8K** while keeping **95% of GPT-4's performance** ([arxiv.org/abs/2406.18665](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.18665)); FrugalGPT (arXiv **2305.05176**) claims **up to 98% cost reduction** matching GPT-4 via cascades ([arxiv.org/abs/2305.05176](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.05176)). But the routing literature itself documents the downside: routers can **"degenerately converge"** onto a single model, collapsing the cost-quality tradeoff (arXiv 2602.03478), and have **life-cycle routing vulnerabilities** where adversarial or drifted inputs get misrouted (arXiv 2503.08704). Practitioner writeups put it plainly: *"Routing on vibes is how a quality regression ships unnoticed"* and *"a drifting verifier is a common way a cascade quietly becomes a cost problem"* ([truefoundry.com](https://www.truefoundry.com/blog/llm-routing-cost-quality-aware-model-selection)). The benchmark-vs-real-cost gap is a named problem: "AI Agents That Matter" (arXiv **2407.01502**) argues SOTA agents are "needlessly complex and costly" and that benchmarks ignoring cost reward overfit, expensive agents ([arxiv.org/abs/2407.01502](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.01502)).

**3. Prompt caching is the one lever that is near-universally shipped, automatic-or-cheap, and with real bill cuts — but it is brittle to prefix non-determinism.** All three frontier vendors ship it: Anthropic at **0.10× read (90% off)** with **1.25× (5-min) / 2.0× (1-hour) write** premiums ([platform.claude.com/docs/.../prompt-caching](https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/build-with-claude/prompt-caching)); OpenAI **automatic, 50% off cached input, no write premium, ≥1024 tokens** ([developers.openai.com/.../prompt-caching](https://developers.openai.com/api/docs/guides/prompt-caching)); Gemini at **0.25× (75% off)** with implicit caching on 2.5 Pro/Flash ([prompthub.us](https://www.prompthub.us/blog/prompt-caching-with-openai-anthropic-and-google-models)). Field reports cite **59%** (ProjectDiscovery), **~90%** (multiple), and a **$720→$72/mo** cut. The failure surface is precise: cache hits require **100% identical prefixes**; a single changing token (timestamp, per-request ID) at or before the breakpoint forces a fresh **cache write every request**; and changing tool definitions or system prompt **invalidates that level and everything after it** (Anthropic's own caveats).

**4. Token-spend budgeting is the least mature lever and the one with no standard.** The dials exist — OpenAI `reasoning_effort` (minimal/low/medium/high, +xhigh on 5.4) where xhigh costs **3–5×** ([openai docs / openrouter](https://openrouter.ai/docs/guides/best-practices/reasoning-tokens)); Anthropic extended-thinking token budgets; GPT-5's internal router auto-switching thinking-vs-fast. But the governance layer (per-team budgets, showback/chargeback, spend caps) is being assembled ad hoc: the **FinOps Foundation stood up a "FinOps for AI" working group** ([finops.org/wg/finops-for-ai-overview](https://www.finops.org/wg/finops-for-ai-overview/)), and the recurring blocker is attribution — *"an LLM API call has no resource to tag — it's a transaction, not an asset"* ([finout.io](https://www.finout.io/blog/genai-cost-allocation-the-complete-guide-for-engineering-and-finance-teams)). Documented failure modes here are **budget caps truncating tasks mid-run** and **reasoning-effort miscalibration** (over-thinking trivial tasks, under-thinking hard ones).

**Honesty caveat for `@pm`:** A large share of the savings numbers below are **vendor- or author-sourced** — RouteLLM/FrugalGPT are **preprints reporting their own benchmarks**; Martian's "20–97% cost cut" and "$1.3B valuation" come from a marketing comparison and a single Medium post; caching "90% / $720→$72" figures are individual practitioner blogs, not audited. Treat all percentage savings as **directional and best-case**, measured on favorable benchmarks, not neutral real-world averages. Pricing multipliers were taken from primary vendor docs (Anthropic/OpenAI) and should still be re-verified before quoting, as model-tier prices move month to month. This note also satisfies the researcher honesty-guard.

## Patterns found

### Pattern 1 — Model right-sizing ladder (strong-for-planning, cheap-for-execution)

- **What it is:** Assign model capability to task difficulty rather than using one model for everything. The canonical shape is a **tiered ladder**: a strong/expensive model does planning, orchestration, and hard reasoning; cheaper/faster models do execution, retrieval, sub-tasks, and routine generation. Two concrete instantiations: (a) **orchestrator-worker** — a lead agent on a flagship model spawns subagents on a mid-tier model running in parallel with isolated context windows; (b) **plan/execute split inside one coding agent** — flagship model for plan mode, mid-tier for the edit/run loop. Anthropic's tiers as of June 2026: **Haiku 4.5 ($1/$5 per MTok), Sonnet 4.6 ($3/$15), Opus 4.7/4.8 ($5/$25)** — a 5× input / 5× output spread between bottom and top rung ([metacto pricing breakdown](https://www.metacto.com/blogs/anthropic-api-pricing-a-full-breakdown-of-costs-and-integration)).
- **Source apps:** **Anthropic multi-agent research system** (Opus 4 lead + Sonnet 4 subagents) ([anthropic.com/engineering/multi-agent-research-system](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/multi-agent-research-system)); **Claude Code** model tiers with documented "Sonnet for daily dev, Opus for SOTA SWE / complex multi-file reasoning" guidance ([cloudzero](https://www.cloudzero.com/blog/claude-pricing/)); **OpenAI** tier ladder (reasoning models vs mini/nano) plus the GPT-5 router that auto-selects between thinking and fast variants.
- **Adoption scale:** First-party in the two largest coding agents (Claude Code, included free in all paid Anthropic plans; OpenAI's tiering). The strongest single data point: a ladder of **Opus 4 lead + Sonnet 4 subagents beat single-agent Opus 4 by 90.2%** on Anthropic's internal research eval, and **token usage alone explained ~80% of the variance** in the improvement ([anthropic.com](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/multi-agent-research-system)).
- **User complaints:** The ladder's cost is the complaint: multi-agent ladders consume **~15× the tokens** of a normal chat, which Anthropic concedes makes them viable "only when the value of the outcome outweighs the expense" ([blog.bytebytego.com summary](https://blog.bytebytego.com/p/how-anthropic-built-a-multi-agent)). Choosing the rung is manual and error-prone — users over-provision Opus for trivial work or under-provision Sonnet/Haiku for hard work.
- **Failure mode:** **Cheap-rung false economy.** Putting the execution rung on a model that is too weak inverts the saving: a task that should take one cycle takes **5–10 self-correction cycles**, and "stronger models reduce iteration overhead rather than total cost — efficiency gains primarily offset higher per-token pricing rather than translating into lower overall cost" ([dr-arsanjani.medium.com](https://dr-arsanjani.medium.com/the-economic-fallacy-of-cheap-ai-the-real-costs-of-agentic-systems-936618305706)). The 15× token multiplier means a mis-rung ladder can be *more* expensive than a single strong model with no quality gain.

### Pattern 2 — LLM routing (per-request strong-vs-cheap selection)

- **What it is:** A lightweight router sits in front of multiple models and decides, **per request**, which model handles it — routing hard/planning prompts to a strong model and routine prompts to a cheap one. Implementations: **trained classifiers** (RouteLLM uses preference data + data augmentation to learn a router between a strong and weak LLM); **semantic routers** (embedding-similarity to route classes of prompts); **gateways with an Auto mode** (OpenRouter's Auto Router calls NotDiamond to pick a model from a curated pool, exposed as a `cost_quality_tradeoff` 0–10 dial, default 7); and **built-in model routers** (GPT-5's real-time autoswitcher between thinking and fast sub-models).
- **Source apps:** **RouteLLM** (LMSYS, OSS, arXiv 2406.18665) ([github.com/lm-sys/RouteLLM](https://github.com/lm-sys/routellm)); **FrugalGPT** cascade (arXiv 2305.05176); **Martian** (commercial, "first LLM router"); **OpenRouter** Auto Router (NotDiamond-powered, 300+ models, OpenAI-compatible API) ([openrouter.ai/blog/insights/model-routing](https://openrouter.ai/blog/insights/model-routing/)); **NotDiamond**; **Unify**; **GPT-5 internal router**.
- **Adoption scale:** RouteLLM reports **>85% cost reduction on MT-Bench, ~45% on MMLU, ~35% on GSM8K** while keeping **95% of GPT-4 performance**, and is fully open-sourced for training/serving/evaluating routers ([lmsys.org/blog/2024-07-01-routellm](https://www.lmsys.org/blog/2024-07-01-routellm/)). FrugalGPT claims **up to 98% cost reduction** matching GPT-4, or **+4% accuracy at the same cost** ([arxiv.org/abs/2305.05176](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.05176)). OpenRouter unifies **300+ models** behind one key; Martian reportedly **nearing a $1.3B valuation** and claims **20–97%** cost cuts ([respan comparison](https://www.respan.ai/market-map/compare/martian-vs-openrouter)). RouterArena exists as an open platform specifically to compare routers (arXiv 2510.00202), signalling the category is mature enough to need standardized benchmarking.
- **User complaints:** Routing requires a way to **measure** quality, which most teams lack — *"routing on quality requires offline eval sets, online LLM-as-judge, or A/B against business metrics"*; without it you are "routing on vibes" ([truefoundry.com](https://www.truefoundry.com/blog/llm-routing-cost-quality-aware-model-selection)). The cost-quality dial is opaque: a higher cost threshold "leads to lower cost but may lead to lower-quality responses," and users can't easily see which requests got down-routed.
- **Failure mode:** **Misroute → silent quality regression.** A hard task sent to the weak model fails quietly because nothing flags it. The preprint literature documents two structural failures: **degenerate convergence**, where routers collapse to always picking one model and lose the tradeoff entirely (arXiv 2602.03478), and **life-cycle routing vulnerabilities**, where drifted or adversarial inputs are systematically misrouted (arXiv 2503.08704). Cascades have a slow-rot variant: **"a drifting verifier is a common way a cascade quietly becomes a cost problem"** ([truefoundry](https://www.truefoundry.com/blog/llm-routing-cost-quality-aware-model-selection)). And the headline savings are **benchmark-conditional** — "AI Agents That Matter" (arXiv 2407.01502) shows cost-blind benchmarks reward agents that overfit and take shortcuts, so router savings measured on MT-Bench/MMLU overstate real-world savings.

### Pattern 3 — Prompt / context caching

- **What it is:** Charge a **discounted rate** for prompt prefixes the model has already processed — system prompts, tool definitions, few-shot examples, long static context (a codebase, a document). The shared mechanic is **prefix matching**: the longest identical leading span of the prompt is served from cache. Two design styles: **explicit** (the caller marks cache breakpoints — Anthropic `cache_control`, Gemini explicit caches) and **automatic** (the platform caches transparently — OpenAI, Gemini implicit).
- **Source apps:** **Anthropic** explicit prompt caching ([platform.claude.com/.../prompt-caching](https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/build-with-claude/prompt-caching)); **OpenAI** automatic prompt caching ([developers.openai.com/.../prompt-caching](https://developers.openai.com/api/docs/guides/prompt-caching)); **Google Gemini** explicit + implicit context caching; **OpenRouter** exposes caching across providers ([openrouter.ai/docs/guides/best-practices/prompt-caching](https://openrouter.ai/docs/guides/best-practices/prompt-caching)).
- **Adoption scale — exact pricing (primary sources):**
  - **Anthropic:** cache **read = 0.10× input (90% discount)**; cache **write = 1.25× (5-min TTL)** or **2.0× (1-hour TTL)**; refresh on use is free; break-even after **one** read (5-min) or **two** reads (1-hour). Minimum cacheable block varies by model (e.g. **1,024 tokens** for Opus 4.8 / Sonnet 4.6; **4,096** for Opus 4.6/4.5 and Haiku 4.5; **512** for Fable/Mythos 5). Caches are **per-workspace isolated** as of Feb 5 2026 ([platform.claude.com](https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/build-with-claude/prompt-caching)).
  - **OpenAI:** **automatic, no code change, 50% off cached input, no write premium**; activates at **≥1,024 tokens**, caches in increments (first ~256-token prefix), lifetime **5–10 min idle up to ~1 hour** (up to **24h** for some gpt-5.x models); all gpt-4o-and-newer ([developers.openai.com](https://developers.openai.com/api/docs/guides/prompt-caching)).
  - **Gemini:** cached tokens at **0.25× (75% discount)**; **implicit** caching on 2.5 Pro/Flash needs no setup ([prompthub.us](https://www.prompthub.us/blog/prompt-caching-with-openai-anthropic-and-google-models)).
  - **Field savings:** ProjectDiscovery reports **59%** LLM-cost cut from caching ([projectdiscovery.io](https://projectdiscovery.io/blog/how-we-cut-llm-cost-with-prompt-caching)); practitioner posts report **~90%** and a **$720→$72/month** reduction ([medium / labeveryday](https://medium.com/@labeveryday/prompt-caching-is-a-must-how-i-went-from-spending-720-to-72-monthly-on-api-costs-3086f3635d63)).
- **User complaints:** The 5-minute default TTL is short for bursty agent fleets — gaps between turns let the cache expire and re-incur a write. Anthropic's **"check `usage` to verify caching occurred — no error is returned"** caveat means silent non-caching; teams discover they paid full price only by auditing token fields. The exact-match requirement is unforgiving.
- **Failure mode:** **Cache miss from non-deterministic prefixes and invalidation cascades.** Anthropic's docs spell out the classic miss: putting a **timestamp or per-request ID at/before the cache breakpoint** changes the hash every request → a fresh **cache write every time** (you pay the 1.25× premium and *never* hit). Invalidation cascades down the hierarchy `tools → system → messages`: **modify a tool definition and the entire cache — tools, system, and messages — is invalidated.** A **20-block lookback window** means a growing conversation can push the breakpoint out of range and silently stop hitting. OpenAI's prefix cache degrades when the same prefix exceeds **~15 req/min** (overflow to other machines). Net: caching that looks configured can quietly contribute **0%** savings.

### Pattern 4 — Deliberate token-spend budgeting (reasoning-effort, thinking budgets, spend caps, four-quadrant value)

- **What it is:** Treat token spend as a **deliberately allocated budget**, not an emergent byproduct. Several distinct controls live under this banner: (a) **reasoning-effort / thinking-budget dials** that cap how much the model "thinks" per request; (b) **per-task token caps / spend caps** that stop a run at a ceiling; (c) **cost-aware agent loops** that prefer cheaper models for sub-tasks within a quality threshold; and (d) the conceptual frame that **token spend has quadrants of value** — i.e., spend should be classified (high-value/low-value × planning/execution, or similar) so budget flows to where it buys outcomes rather than burning on low-value loops. The vault slug `concept-token-spend-has-four-quadrants` names this last idea; the *adjacent shipped controls* are the reasoning dials and spend caps below.
- **Source apps:** **OpenAI `reasoning_effort`** — `minimal | low | medium | high` (and `xhigh` on GPT-5.4), plus a `verbosity` low/medium/high control; `none` behaves like a non-thinking model (cheapest), `xhigh` costs **3–5×** ([nxcode GPT-5.4 guide](https://www.nxcode.io/resources/news/gpt-5-4-api-developer-guide-reasoning-computer-use-2026); [openrouter reasoning tokens](https://openrouter.ai/docs/guides/best-practices/reasoning-tokens)). **Anthropic extended-thinking** token budgets. **GPT-5 internal router** auto-selecting thinking-vs-fast. **Spend-guardrail tooling**: Oracle "runtime budget guardrails for agentic AI" ([blogs.oracle.com](https://blogs.oracle.com/ai-and-datascience/runtime-budget-guardrails-agentic-ai)); cost-guardrails-for-agent-fleets writeups ([medium / Micheal-Lanham](https://medium.com/@Micheal-Lanham/cost-guardrails-for-agent-fleets-how-to-prevent-your-ai-agents-from-burning-through-your-budget-ea68722af3fe)).
- **Adoption scale:** `reasoning_effort` is shipped across the entire GPT-5 family and is a first-class API parameter; OpenRouter normalizes reasoning-token controls across providers. The budgeting/guardrail layer is earlier-stage — assembled by FinOps tooling vendors rather than standardized.
- **User complaints:** The dials are hard to calibrate — "accuracy improves from Minimal to Medium" but reasoning tokens and runtime "increase significantly" with each step, so users guess. GPT-5's *automatic* router drew direct complaints that the model "suddenly got dumber" when the router silently down-routed reasoning ([anandchowdhary.com](https://anandchowdhary.com/notes/2025/gpt5-didnt-suddenly-get-dumber-the-router-did)) — i.e. an invisible spend-budget decision degraded output without the user choosing it.
- **Failure mode:** **Budget caps truncate mid-task; reasoning-effort miscalibration wastes or starves.** A hard token cap that fires mid-run leaves a half-finished task that must be re-run (often re-paying for the truncated work). Setting `reasoning_effort` too low **under-thinks** hard problems (silent quality loss); too high **over-thinks** trivial ones (3–5× waste at xhigh). And the automatic version (GPT-5 router) makes the spend decision *for* the user, who then experiences unexplained quality swings — the failure is loss of predictability, not just loss of money.

### Pattern 5 — FinOps for LLMs (dashboards, per-team budgets, showback/chargeback)

- **What it is:** Apply cloud-FinOps discipline — **visibility, attribution, optimization, accountability** — to token spend. Concrete artifacts: **cost dashboards** broken down by team/feature/model; **per-team or per-feature budgets** with threshold alerts and anomaly detection; **showback** (show teams their spend to build awareness) vs **chargeback** (actually bill it back for accountability); and **metadata tagging** at the application layer to attribute each call to a team/product/feature, propagated through to billing data.
- **Source apps:** **FinOps Foundation "FinOps for AI"** working group / framework ([finops.org/wg/finops-for-ai-overview](https://www.finops.org/wg/finops-for-ai-overview/)); commercial tooling — **Finout** (GenAI cost allocation, per-feature/per-team chargeback, anomaly detection) ([finout.io](https://www.finout.io/blog/genai-cost-allocation-the-complete-guide-for-engineering-and-finance-teams)); **TrueFoundry** (metadata tagging, team budgets, chargeback reports, 3-layer rate-limit gateway) ([truefoundry.com](https://www.truefoundry.com/blog/llm-cost-attribution-team-budgets)); **Cloudchipr**, **Spheron** (GPU FinOps, tag-based budgeting).
- **Adoption scale:** The FinOps Foundation — the established cloud-FinOps standards body — standing up a dedicated AI working group is the adoption signal that this has crossed from niche to standardizing. The commercial tooling layer (Finout, TrueFoundry, Cloudchipr) indicates paying enterprise demand, though no neutral install/seat counts were found.
- **User complaints:** Attribution is the core pain: **"an LLM API call has no resource to tag — it's a transaction, not an asset"**, so teams must capture metadata at the app layer and propagate it to billing — work the cloud-cost playbook never required ([finout.io](https://www.finout.io/blog/genai-cost-allocation-the-complete-guide-for-engineering-and-finance-teams)). Showback builds awareness but "drives weaker accountability"; chargeback drives accountability but "requires more precise allocation infrastructure" most teams don't have ([finout.io agentic cost governance](https://www.finout.io/blog/agentic-ai-cost-governance-controlling-spend-before-it-controls-you)).
- **Failure mode:** **Cost-allocation gaps and the cloud-playbook breakdown.** Cloudchipr's framing is explicit: "your cloud cost playbook breaks down with AI" because token/transaction pricing doesn't map to taggable assets ([cloudchipr.com](https://cloudchipr.com/blog/finops-for-ai)). Without app-layer tagging, agent-fleet spend lands in an untagged bucket no team owns — and anomaly alerts fire after the spend, not before. Combined with Pattern 4's mid-task truncation, naive budget caps can both overspend (no attribution) and break work (hard caps).

## Unmet needs observed

- **No standard for "spend has quadrants of value."** The vault's `concept-token-spend-has-four-quadrants` framing — classify spend by value before allocating — has no shipped product equivalent. Reasoning-effort dials and spend caps are *blunt* (per-request or per-run ceilings); none classify *whether the spend is buying a high-value outcome*. The closest is NotDiamond's `cost_quality_tradeoff` dial, which is one-dimensional.
- **Quality measurement is the missing prerequisite for safe routing.** Every routing failure mode traces back to teams not having an eval set / LLM-judge / A/B harness to detect down-route regressions. Routing is shipped widely; the regression-detection layer under it is not.
- **Cache-miss observability is poor.** Vendors return *no error* on a cache miss; teams discover non-caching only by manually auditing `usage` fields. There is no standard "you thought this was cached and it wasn't" alert.
- **Mid-task budget enforcement vs. graceful degradation.** Hard caps truncate; there is no widely shipped "soft cap → downshift model / compress context / checkpoint and resume" pattern that respects a budget without destroying in-flight work.
- **Benchmark-to-real-cost translation.** Router/cascade savings are reported on MT-Bench/MMLU/GSM8K; "AI Agents That Matter" shows these overstate real-world savings, but no standard exists for reporting cost on held-out, cost-controlled, real workloads.
- **Per-agent (not just per-team) attribution for fleets.** FinOps tooling attributes to teams/features; agent fleets need per-agent / per-task showback to find which agent is burning budget, which the "transaction-not-an-asset" problem makes hard.
